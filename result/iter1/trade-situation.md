## SITUATION SUMMARY
The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since March 4, 2026, following
Operation Epic Fury (US–Israel airstrikes on Iran on Feb 28) and Iran's declaration
of closure [Source 3]. A fragile US–Iran ceasefire has been in place since April 7
and was extended April 21, but Monday's UAE port attacks — the largest escalation
since the ceasefire — sent Brent surging +5.8% to $114.44/bbl [Source 5]. As of
today (May 5), Brent has pulled back ~4% to $109.87/bbl after US Defense Secretary
Hegseth confirmed the ceasefire remains in place [Source 5]. Diplomatic progress
is stalled: Iran's latest proposal to reopen Hormuz without a concurrent nuclear deal
was rebuffed by Washington [Source 6]. Bypass routes (Saudi Petroline, UAE
Habshan–Fujairah pipeline, Iraq–Ceyhan) are operating at or near capacity, covering
an estimated 7–8 mb/d of the ~15 mb/d that previously transited Hormuz [Sources
4, 7, 8] — leaving a structural supply gap that keeps prices elevated even with
SPR releases underway.
## RAG STRUCTURAL CONTEXT
The Saudi East-West Pipeline (Petroline) has a nameplate capacity of 4.8 mb/d at
standard utilization, expandable to ~7 mb/d when NGL lines are converted to crude;
historically only ~50% was utilized pre-crisis [RAG: Oil 101, Ch.11]. The
UAE Habshan–Fujairah pipeline provides a ~1.8 mb/d Hormuz bypass under normal
conditions [RAG: Oil 101, Ch.11]. Tanker freight from the Arab Gulf is benchmarked
against TD3C (Middle East Gulf–China) and TD15 (West Africa–China/Cape route),
both denominated in Worldscale points and cleared via the Baltic Exchange [RAG:
Oil 101, Ch.11].
